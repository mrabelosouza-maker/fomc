# Deregulating in a Financial Boom: What Could Go Wrong? — barr — 2026-06-06

Governor Michael S. Barr, at American University, Washington, D.C., June 6, 2026.

Governor Barr expresses concern that recent regulatory changes are weakening bank oversight. He emphasizes that while banks need room to grow, history demonstrates that without proper safeguards, banks striving to innovate in pursuit of higher profits may take excessive risks.

The speech identifies three major deregulatory trends:

Capital Requirements: The Federal Reserve has reduced capital requirements for the largest banks by approximately 6 percent—equivalent to $60 billion less in capital to protect against bank failure. Barr notes these reductions move capital standards toward the low end of the optimal range established by academic research.

Supervision Weakening: Changes to the rating system for large financial institutions constitute grade inflation that would allow poorly managed banks to be judged as well managed. The number of supervisory matters requiring attention had already fallen to roughly half compared to 2024 levels.

Consumer Protection Declines: Financial consumer protections have been scaled back, risking conditions similar to those preceding the Global Financial Crisis.

Historical Context: Barr references major crises—the Great Depression, savings and loan crisis, and 2008 financial crisis—arguing that research demonstrates reducing financial regulatory requirements can and often does produce financial stress. He compares deregulation to purchasing less insurance, noting asymmetric costs: modestly high capital requirements impose small growth costs, while inadequate requirements create sharply rising costs to financial stability.

Research Findings: Studies indicate bank capital ratios between 15-23 percent of risk-weighted assets would likely prevent historical banking crises. The speech concludes that deregulation's short-term sugar high is outweighed by longer-term risks to economic stability and growth.
