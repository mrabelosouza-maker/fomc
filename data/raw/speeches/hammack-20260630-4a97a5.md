# CNBC Interview at the ECB Sintra Forum — hammack — 2026-06-30

## Highlights

Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack, interviewed by CNBC's Sarah Eisen on the sidelines of the ECB forum in Central Portugal (Sintra), confirms she is one of the more hawkish voices on the FOMC and says inflation that has been too high for five years may require higher interest rates.

---

**Sarah Eisen:** You have really distinguished yourself lately at the Federal Reserve as being one of the more hawkish voices in terms of policy. Is that accurate? Is that fair?

**Beth Hammack:** Well, I just look at where the data is. And what I see right now is that there’s no tension in our mandate. I see a labor market that’s right around my estimate of full employment. Growth numbers look good. We’ve got stability in that employment number. And we’ve got inflation that’s too high. And it’s been too high for the past five years. And so when I look at policy, if that continues, it may mean that we need higher interest rates to bring inflation back down to target.

**Sarah Eisen:** So would you be in favor of raising rates as soon as the July meeting?

**Beth Hammack:** I keep an open mind walking into every meeting. I think every meeting is a live meeting. And it’s important to look at the data and see where that’s taking us.

**Sarah Eisen:** But for you, that’s taking you to higher rates.

**Beth Hammack:** We’ve got some time before the next meeting, and so I want to wait and see how all of that data comes in before I make a decision. I’m not going to prejudge anything.

**Sarah Eisen:** Some people look at these higher inflation numbers and they say a lot of it is due to the energy price spike, which is temporary. We’re already trading at levels down to the low 70s on WTI, so that should reverse. So how do you make sure you don’t overreact?

**Beth Hammack:** Energy has certainly been one component of the inflationary pressures that we’ve seen, and energy impacts headline inflation. And I try to look at both headline inflation and core inflation, which takes out the more volatile energy and food prices. And when I look at that core inflation, that’s been very elevated as well. That’s continuing to persist with a three handle. When I’m out in the district talking to businesses, what I hear are inflation pressures, yes, coming from energy and coming from some of the supply-chain disruptions with the closed Strait of Hormuz, but I also hear about pressures coming more broadly from the AI data-center buildup. I’m hearing about pressures from insurance. I’m hearing about electricity pressures. And so, to me, what I see is a more broad-based picture of inflation pressure.

**Sarah Eisen:** Wouldn’t you see it, though, in wages too, if it was real underlying inflation that the Fed needed to fight?

**Beth Hammack:** Not necessarily. The Phillips curve is certainly one of the tools that we use to think about our models. But right now, what I’m seeing is core services. That’s a number that’s continued to stay high, even though we have wage inflation and wage pressures that are kind of in line with historical norms. They’re not showing us significant signs of pressure.

**Sarah Eisen:** Do you worry, though, about risking that robust labor market and consumer by hiking interest rates?

**Beth Hammack:** I do. I certainly think about what’s happening on the consumer side. What I’ve seen from the consumer is that consumer spending has held up really well, even through these higher energy prices. We talk to some of the banks in the district who share their credit-card spending. The spend for gasoline has gone up, but the discretionary spend has been constant. People have been using the higher tax refunds. Now, with gas prices back down, there’s not really going to be any tension on that spending side. And so I want to watch that consumer data. And if it continues to hold up, then that says to me that policy may not be restrictive enough to help us bring inflation back down.

**Sarah Eisen:** Do you think policy is restrictive right now in any place? You do see it in housing.

**Beth Hammack:** You see it in housing. That’s about the only place that you see it. You see it more because of the historical rates and the lock-in issues, because so many people locked in their mortgages at these low rates when we were in a 0% rate environment. The economy is doing reasonably well right now. You see pretty good growth numbers. We see stability in the labor market, and inflation is still persisting well above target.

**Sarah Eisen:** You said AI is inflationary, because a lot of people think it’s disinflationary.

**Beth Hammack:** Well, there could be impacts in both directions. What I’m seeing right now is that the spending, the CapEx build, that is having inflationary pressures right now. We have a number of different manufacturers in the district, a number of whom are contributing to the AI buildout. They do electric switching that’s heavily used in these data centers. And what they say is that the demand is insatiable, that these hyperscalers will pay almost any price for those inputs, and they need things built yesterday. I was talking to one manufacturer who was saying that they’ve had 13 years’ worth of production orders all in one year. And so when I look broadly, particularly around large companies, I’m not seeing a lot of restraint in the economy. I’m not hearing from these businesses that interest rates or credit spreads are a reason why they’re holding back from investment and growth.

(Source: CNBC interview with Sarah Eisen, ECB Forum on Central Banking, Sintra, Portugal, 2026-06-30. Hammack is a voting FOMC member in 2026.)
