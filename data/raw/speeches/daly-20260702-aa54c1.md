# Banco de España Panel: Shocks, Uncertainty, and Policy Divergence

**Speaker:** Mary C. Daly, President, Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco
**Date:** 2026-07-02
**Venue:** Banco de España panel (central-bank forum, Portugal)

## Opening remarks: shocks, uncertainty and policy divergence

I will just second what you've said. I think those are the same topics that I heard myself. I want to add things to it so that I'm being additive here as opposed to just saying the same thing a different way.

The one thing that I walked away with, so I go there and as Maria said, there's a sense that the world is uncertain. As Jose said, there's uncertainty. There's so many shocks coming our way, not really sure how the shocks will play out in different places.

But there's a recognition that as policymakers, as central bankers, we're all trying to achieve the same thing, which is use our old tools and history and combine them with how the world might behave differently as we move forward for a variety of reasons.

One, all the countries, even though we're faced by similar events and we face the same uncertainty, we are in different places. We have different rates of growth at this point. We have different ways we've recovered from the pandemic. We have different previous strengths. And it's not just about the institutions are different. It's the economies have responded different to the various shocks that we've faced.

And so as policymakers, we can't just look to each other and say, what did you do? I'll do it. You have to think how your policy is specific for your nation, which is what we always do. But there's a sense sometimes when there's a global shock that everyone responds.

The pandemic was going to be one of those that everyone just puts all their resources to it. It was about the amounts and timing, but it was all resources to it. Now, you know, the ECB just raised rates. Other central banks are holding steady. Some are in different places. Some have different time frames over which they make decisions. And I found that in many ways reassuring.

And President Lagarde said something that I really took away in her remark during the policy panel. She said that this shouldn't be misread as divergence in thinking. I think Andrew Bailey said first this should be read as we're all facing the same shocks. We have very different starting points and those starting points matter.

So I think you'll see more and more places where policy decisions could be different, but policy motivations, the way we think about things, will be identical: price stability; in the United States' case, full employment; and how do we navigate that.

And as they just talked about, we have AI, a technology shock. We have, for many countries, including the U.S., the tariff shocks, the oil price shocks, the uncertainty about whether we're really going to stay around $70 a barrel now or whether something else will happen, how quickly these shocks will dissipate. Are they really, as we've traditionally looked at them as economists, simply look-through shocks because they're not meant to last forever? Or do they have some fundamental way in which they bleed into the economy and change things?

And then now there's the new, in the United States anyway, investment shock of AI, which is competing for resources in a time frame where people are wondering, not knowing yet, whether that itself will be inflationary. And we'll be in this horse race between the investments creating a demand shock, but the supply shock, the positive supply shock, not being quite in front of us yet.

And truth is, we don't know the answers to these questions. And trying to think about them with the patience that central banking requires: if you act too quickly, you can be premature to bridle things that you shouldn't. And if you act too slowly, you can let inflation take off in a way that would be unwelcome to our citizens.

So those are the balancing acts I think we're all facing. And I really thought that the conference was a great way to have everybody together. There's a sense of camaraderie when you're all there with other central bankers from across the globe, not just from the ECB, obviously, and all talking.

## AI and San Francisco

Oh, I think about AI all the time. For so many reasons. You know, we started a center on emerging tech, thinking about AI in particular, in January 2023, largely because we could see it already peeking into businesses, not just of the companies that were developing the LLMs and the technology, but also businesses that were starting to use it in the services industry and manufacturing.

But at that point, we were talking about a 10-year window. That's what companies were telling us: "Oh, in 10 years, five years, we'll see something from it." That has accelerated at a rate of speed that I haven't seen in a technology before.

I think you have to go back in the United States to something like electrification. Once we started it up, electrification took 100 years, but the end part of that curve, there was a very flat part and an exponential rise in how electricity shaped the world and how it created productivity growth. And that seems a little bit like what we're seeing.

I think we're in the very early stages of this, not at the point where we're seeing the productivity gains yet. But the really interesting thing I think maybe all of you will find is that I didn't want to be so San Francisco-centric. That's certainly where you can find the enthusiasts, but I was worried that I was missing out on the doomsayers. Where were the people who were very negative about this?

So, if you drew a continuum, there'd be the doomsayers and the enthusiasts, and there were very few people in the middle. Now, there's a lot of people in the middle. And I don't mean just consumers. I mean firms.

So I have the nine states in the western United States. Those are states that we define as the intermountain, like Utah, Nevada, places where you might think Rocky Mountains, but you don't necessarily think technology hubs. And then we have Alaska and Hawaii as well and all the coastal states.

And so I started traveling. I always traveled to these locations, spent a lot of time doing this. Where was I seeing AI really pop up in terms of using and manufacturing? In those states that weren't the makers of the models, they were the users of the models.

And they were first using it for, how do I get some cost reductions? Remember back then, it was a very tight labor market in the United States. People were calling it frothy. People were changing jobs faster than they were staying at jobs. So they were using technology to try to fight that.

Then came the, we can do a lot more than we ever imagined. This isn't just going to be an iPhone. This is going to be something bigger. So we went to that, and now people are imagining a world where the innovations that they've tried to make in their companies over time actually can be assisted in creating those idea spaces by AI itself.

So we're a long way from having this be everywhere in all companies. We're a long way from us being past the early adopters, but there's a sufficient number of early adopters. You're starting to see the competitors follow along, and I think that's the excitement.

You know, technology has never created productivity growth on a sustainable basis if it was a narrow set. It was when it got into the businesses, it spread, fully diffused, and people started changing their business process in order to have a long-term gain.

So I'm starting to see that very early. Okay, so we're not in baseball land. We are in football land. And it is the World Cup after all. Does Spain play tomorrow?

Oh, good. So I'll have a chance of watching the game. I can't stay till midnight. So that's good. But either way, we're in the very early stages of the match.

And if you saw the Senegal-Belgium match last evening, we watched it just as part of the ether of being in Portugal. I thought Senegal had so many chances and hadn't quite made it, they were going to surely win. And I got up this morning to realize that was completely different.

So I guess what I'm saying with this is that we can't forecast what AI will do, and anyone who says they can is overstating the case, in my judgment. But we can definitely start writing down what would we need to see to suggest that this has a lot of legs under it, and what would we see that suggests it's starting to cool off.

I haven't seen any signs of cooling, and I've seen lots of suggestions that legs are forming under it.

## U.S. outlook: AI investment, consumers, labor market, inflation and forward guidance

Sure. So I'll start where, you know, as we've already talked about, a modal or the most likely outcome is not the place any central banker wants to live right now. It's just not a good place.

So let me not give a modal outlook. Let me talk about the factors that will shape the outlook. And I do believe completely in the scenarios. And even at this point, you would have to have a full matrix of permutations to understand all the scenarios we face.

But you can definitely think, okay, these are the factors that are going to cause the evolution of the economy in any country. So let me talk about the United States.

I see the factors as we have very strong investment growth, exceedingly strong investment growth. And this investment growth is in AI. And it's driven in a lot about physical construction, but also investment in software — not software so much, but AI equipment. So all the things that go through that. And that's really computers and IT equipment. And that's very strong.

And I don't see any sense in which that's going to start coming down unless there's a sort of a hard stop in this, because people are excited about this. It's not just the model builders. It's the model users, and you're getting some momentum. That's really positive.

The second thing that's happened is, as I said, you just don't see any signs of lack of resiliency in our economy. So consumers, despite the fact that they've been dealing with high inflation, above-target inflation for five years, they're just staying in the game.

They're not the main driver of our growth. The investment side is, but they're not leaving either. And so that's a really good sign for our economy. And I think it reflects in part that people still have employment. You know, that's a big part of how people spend their money: if they have good labor earnings, they can spend.

So when you go to the job market, it's steady. We had a little bit of concern last year that it might be softening, but now it's stabilized. We'll wait for the jobs numbers in just a few — half an hour or something like that — for the U.S. But one data point is not going to make or break our analysis of this. It really has, across a variety of measures, looked to be steady.

So those are good, really good ingredients. Of course, we have inflation still printing above target. In fact, inflation's risen on the back of some of the tariff effects and also the oil price shock. But now with oil prices back down to nearer 70 and futures markets thinking they'll stay there, that has some hope for relief.

And that's what we just need right now. It looks pretty good if you're looking as a central banker. For me, I say this is good for the United States growth rate. It's good for the U.S. labor market. And we continue to have policy in a slightly restricted position, so inflation should come down.

And there's all these risks. Everything I just said — AI investment, consumers' resiliency, the fact that oil is down to 70, the fact that we expect tariff inputs or shocks to roll off — all of that has a question mark behind it. Uncertainty bands around that still remain fairly large.

So I would say that's why I think there's a scenario where we have to fight inflation that turns out to be more persistent. There's also a scenario where the growth just doesn't continue to sustain itself because there's something that pulls out either consumers or the investment slows because people are more worried and they haven't seen the gains yet.

Those are not more likely than the last one, but they are the risks that we face. And so that's an outlook that I will take relative to some of the challenges we've come through. But it's not an outlook that just means we can reflexively decide right now to give forward guidance.

That's why you see a lot of central bankers not moving away from the principle. I will speak for myself: I don't move away from the principle about forward guidance. I understand its importance in monetary policy. But the worst kind of guidance you can give is certain guidance in an uncertain world. And so that would be false guidance and not forward guidance.

And that's why I'm really focused on reaction functions and also how I see the shaping of the economy and the outlook — not the outlook itself or anything about policy specifically.

## Housing affordability in the U.S.

No, there's no way I will be able to summarize the U.S. experience in housing. As you know, we're a large and very diverse country in many ways.

But there is a unifying theme in housing right now that you might not expect unless you have visited or go there. And this was confirmed by — you can see this when you go anywhere in the United States. You'll see people talk about housing affordability.

And they don't mean for low-income workers. They mean across the board. They also don't have any — they're not particularly geographically represented, or it doesn't matter what political party you're from, what age you are, how much education you have. It really is across the board.

So this was confirmed, a think tank did a study of this. It's about three years old at this point, but it was striking. They did it by race, ethnicity, education, gender, age in five-year age groups, location, religious preference, political preference and income. And across the board, housing was considered the number one problem that the U.S. faces.

And so you start to dig into that. What you learn is it's really housing demand outstripping housing supply. But that's for parents or grandparents who want to live next to their kids to see their grandchildren and they can't move, or the kids can't live in the location their parents raised them because it's become too expensive.

And then mobility feels more limited. So people don't feel like they can move where the jobs are because sometimes they can't find a house. And then people who have small families and now want to have a bigger family, or their family is just growing in size, they can't move up because there's not the right size property.

So I think it has meant that it is now sitting where it needs to sit, which is at the federal government, at the state governments, not just at the local governments. And hopefully, that focus will bring some relief.

But what I find striking is that whether you're in Spain or other parts of Europe or the United States, these challenges are very similar. And I think economists have done a lot of work to trace it back to some of the elements. This goes all the way back to the financial crisis, when housing was, at least in the United States, significantly affected.

And after that, the industry just doesn't — the dynamics of it aren't the same. And then, of course, that's got all their competing things like AI data centers, et cetera. But really, it's all the way back to there. That's where, in the United States, the wedge started to form between housing demand and housing supply. And every year, it's gotten a little worse.

## Measurement, mandate and central bank tools

So the way I think about it, maybe I'll frame it this way, is that we have two mandates: price stability, full employment in the United States. Those are unmovable. Those are mandates given to us by Congress. It was part of our formation, so those are absolutely set.

The way I think of the question is, does a new world with new challenges demand new tools, techniques, data? Yes, always.

I wouldn't think of this as a new thing. I would think of this as something — and I've worked at the Federal Reserve as a young economist coming in a long time ago — and every single year, researchers and conferences like these and people who work for the central bank and also people who work outside of it keep pushing us to do our jobs more effectively.

So on inflation, yes, we should look under every stone and take an interest in where we can get better data, more data, and analyze it better, look at history not only in our own country, with other countries, absolutely. And for employment, the same thing.

But that doesn't mean that we move the goalposts of our mandates. First of all, we can't. And second of all, that is very bad for credibility and trust.

The best tool, in my opinion, that the Federal Reserve has, or a central bank has, is the trust of its people who we're meant to serve. We exist to serve, in our case, the American people. And if we say, well, we're not there, so let's move it, then I don't think that's very good for our credibility or our trust.

So no, we're not moving the goalposts. But we definitely will continue to study, modernize, and do better tomorrow than we did today.

## Audience question: AI investment, financing and public debt

So, you know, one thing that may not be known: it's certainly true that the big investment companies are starting to borrow, but most of their work is financed through earnings, their cash flows. And so they're just taking that and investing it in this.

And it actually represents not the largest part of their business. These are large, large companies with lots of capabilities, and they're putting a lot of investment into these data centers, which I would say are not just funding the competition to make better models. That's a fraction of what they do.

As we talked about earlier, a lot of it is to support the cloud and putting the data together and making it accessible so that you can do these things. So I haven't seen that as a major concern yet.

You know, and we also know that it will — or we hope and actually increasingly believe that it will — create productivity down the road. It's always going to be about this timing. What do we have today and what are the effects of it, especially in the monetary policymaking window? And then what do we expect in the future and how long is that future away?

So I haven't seen that yet in the United States, largely because these companies have very large capacities and demand for what they're creating seems insatiable at this point.

## Audience question: AI valuations, China and competition

I don't think that's a new risk. It's not like it's an emerging risk. It's just when you have things that are growing as quickly as they are, that as much enthusiasm is in there as they are, then there's a risk of things happening.

I don't think it's about the risk. It's about the likelihood, or any evidence that you're seeing things that would make that risk move to the front of your desk, as opposed to being that second level of papers you have — that are, here are the risks and here's how we watch them.

So very much like José just said, we monitor this. So we have a large monitor about financial stability, and leverage matters a lot. But when most of it's financed out of equity and in cash flows, those are things that are helpful for us to see. If that changes, then that's really what can matter. And so we have to monitor that.

And then I think the competition is, of course, healthy. It makes the product better and leads to further gains in the productivity growth that we're all looking for.

So I don't think it's risky to have competition. It's actually part of what you should expect. And whether it's coming from China or other countries, this sort of pressure to do better, not just in the building of the models, but also in the delivery of outcomes from the models, is really what will create the value that is behind some of the enthusiasm.

## Audience question: speed of change and timing of central bank decisions

Well, I'm not going to speak for the Federal Reserve here, and certainly anything like that hasn't been discussed.

But what I would say is underlying the reason that that's not the top of the list is because we're looking 18 months — you know, six, 10, 12, 18 months ahead.

The speed at which things change is less about the monetary policy decisions today and more about the speed at which we have to learn, study, understand, debate, and come up with, okay, what do we think is the best risk management strategy now when we have uncertainty?

So the speed of change just means everyone who works in the central bank is extraordinarily busy studying these things. But it doesn't mean — in fact, I would suggest it's the opposite of what you want to do. You don't want to react quickly when the world is changing quickly. You want to assess before you jump or act, because you'll make better decisions.

And so there's always this pull as a human to say, let's go fix that, let's go fix that. But then you're just chasing balls of yarn like cats. And you may not step back far enough and say, what is the real lay of the land? How does this affect not just the temporary situation we're in, but the structural dynamics of the economy, the longer-term divergences from the data and our goals?

And so I'm much more of a gradualist when it comes to these things, and taking it slowly to do it well.
