# Brief Remarks on the Economic Outlook and Monetary Policy — barr — 2026-03-26

Governor Michael S. Barr, at the Brookings Institution, Washington, D.C., March 26, 2026.

The Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) met last week and chose to maintain the current setting of monetary policy—a decision I supported.

The U.S. economy has thus far remained resilient, despite having experienced a series of shocks over the past year. The backdrop for these developments has been an economy that has continued to grow at a solid pace, supported by resilient consumer spending, substantial productivity growth over the past several years, and exceptionally strong business investment in artificial intelligence (AI) and data centers.

The latest of these developments has been the current conflict in the Middle East, which has affected both oil production and transportation in much of the region, driving up energy prices and affecting other commodities as well. Another price shock could increase longer-term inflation expectations. Tariffs have driven up goods prices. A recent Supreme Court ruling reduced the effective tariff rate to approximately 10 percent, though uncertainty remains about the ultimate inflation impacts.

Core inflation, which excludes volatile food and energy prices and is a good guide to future inflation, likely was 3 percent in February, about where it was a year ago. Nonhousing services inflation has remained elevated.

On the labor market: net immigration cuts and declining participation have slowed labor force growth to near zero. Job creation has also been close to zero for the past year, a highly unusual experience outside of a recession. We are in a "low hire, low fire" environment.

On regulation, Barr expressed dissent regarding recent regulatory changes, stating they weaken banking system resiliency and erode trust in financial stability measures.
